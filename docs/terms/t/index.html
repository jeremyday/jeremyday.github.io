T

Technicality​

A description of how difficult a trail is to run on. A trail with low technicality will generally be smooth dirt with few rocks and roots. More technical trails will have rocks, roots, mud, sand, snow, or other obstacles requiring your attention. The more attention you have to pay to where you put your feet (see two rules) the more mentally fatigued you get over the course of a race, and the more mentally fatigued you get the more likely you are to make a simple mistake that could drastically hurt your race. For instance, you might forget to change out of wet socks, which could lead to blisters if not taken care of, which could lead to a lot of foot pain later in a race, which could lead to missing a cutoff, which could lead to a DNF.

Trail Marking​

The system the race director uses to indicate to runners they are on the correct trail. Usually this is colored ribbons tied to branches or stakes stuck in the ground. For races with a night running component this might also include special reflective ribbons to reflect the light from flashlights or headlamps. Often the race director will add special signage to indicate runners are going the wrong way, that they need to make a turn, or that they should be wary of potential dangerous conditions ahead.

The quality of trail marking can vary widely between races. Some races have a system in place and fantastic markings so the runners know they are on the right trail at all times. Other races, however, have pretty thin markings, leaving runners sometimes uncertain as to whether they are going the right way. There can be a lot of anxiety in a race with poor trail markings, because no runner likes the feeling that they may not be on the actual race course.

Trail Miles​

Any trail distance published by any race director is going to be approximate. A 5k on trails will never be exactly 3.1 miles. The Western States 100 is about as close as any 100 miler gets to actual distance, and it's 100.2 miles.

Training Race​

I like to run an ultra or ultras as training for a 100 mile race. It gives me a chance to gauge my fitness and dial in my gear. I think of these as supported long runs. If I can get one in a cool location that's a huge bonus.

Two Rules​

I tell new trail runners there are really just two primary rules in trail running: pay attention to where you put your feet and pick your feet up. Fail either of those and you might end up on your face eating trail dirt.
