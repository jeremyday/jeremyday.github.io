Waistlamp​

Just a headlamp I wear around my waist. Most headlamps have enough strap that I can make it stretch to fit. I find this works better for me than a headlamp for a couple of reasons. First, the light is low enough that it casts shadows on the trail, which gives me enough indication of what I'm running over to know that I'm lifting my feet high enough to clear obstacles (see the two rules of trail running). Second, I can look away from the cone of light, which you cannot do with a headlamp. It gives my eyes a bit of a rest.

Walking​

My first ultra (and first marathon) was the Rocky Raccoon 50k in November of 2009. In that race I was able to run the first 26.2 miles, but then a bum IT band started really slowing me down (that's a long story for another day). I also ran out of water, and given Texas can be pretty warm even in November I was pretty parched and depressed by the time I walked into the final aid station at mile 28. I was ready to drop, and I knew the start/finish line was a short walk down the road from the aid station. When I told the two women and the grizzled trail veteran volunteers that I was dropping they managed to talk me into finishing. First they gave me a cup of Coke and a banana rolled in salt. The salty banana was every bit as awful as it sounds, but the combination of salt and potassium was just what I needed to get back to running. And then the grizzled trail veteran, who in my head looks and sounds like Sam Elliot, leaned over the table and says the three words that probably changed my life. "Son, walkin's finishin'." I walked. I finished.

Ultra Quote

Son, walkin's finishin'.

-- Grizzled Trail Veteran

Water Bottle​

A handheld, very minimal form of hydration on the go. I've been using something similar to the Nathan QuickSqueeze for many, many years. These are great for when you don't really need a full hydration pack. However, they can wear your arm out, so you need to train with it to build up those bottle-holding muscles.

Water Crossing​

Often during a trail race you will be called upon to cross some water. Sometimes it's a trickle and barely worth the name "water crossing". Sometimes you can step on stones to make it across without getting wet, while other times there may be a log or some sort of bridge over the water. Other times, however, you are going to get your feet wet. Be prepared for it. The stream or riverbed when you cross might be slippery, so step carefully. Poles can be very useful for keeping your balance when crossing water. Read the course description the race director publishes and know when you might expect water crossings. Or, more specifically, know when the aid stations are after the water crossing. You may want to consider stashing a dry pair of socks in a drop bag or carry some on your person. Wet socks can make your run seriously unpleasant and could lead to hot spots or chafing.

Western States 100 has a famous water crossing at about mile 78. This is a serious, long, waist-deep crossing of the American River, and during the race there is a rope stretched across the river that runners and pacers** hold on to as they cross. You may be sleepy by mile 78 of the race, but cold river water will wake you up in a hurry.

The Ozark Trail 100 does not have any particularly long or deep stream crossing, but it has numerous calf-deep crossings. The race is in November, so the water is quite cold, and particularly overnight can lead to hypothermia if you aren't careful.
