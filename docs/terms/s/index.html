Safety Runner​

A special kind of pacer who specifically is required to run behind you. A traditional pacer can run in front of you, which can encourage you to run faster than you might otherwise run. A safety runner is specifically just to keep you, the runner, safe, while not encouraging you to run faster by being in front.

Shoes​

The single most important thing you need for your races. Pick your shoes wisely. For a 100 miler you probably should get shoes at least a half size larger than normal, but a full size might be best. Feet can swell during a race, so you want some room to expand. Also, if you're running a mountain race having some extra room in the front of the toe box can help keep your toes from smashing into the end of the toe box. Trust me when I say you do not want hamburger toes during a race.

One thing you'll need to determine is if you really need trail shoes or if you can get by with road shoes. It really depends on the race and the degree of technicality. If you're running a road race like the Spartathlon (seriously, check it out) or Badwater (not my cup of tea, but a lot of people seem to be into it) then you likely don't need trail shoes. If you're hitting trails there are as many different kinds of shoes as there are conditions you might encounter: shoes for sandy trails, shoes for mud, shoes for rocks, shoes for snow, and everything in between.

In addition to shoes for different types of trails there are shoes of varying weights and degrees of flexibility. Shoes tend to be minimalist, normal, or what I call maximalist. I lean very heavily on the minimalist side, with Inov-8 being my minimal shoe of choice. They tend to be light, flexible, and have little in the way of padding. On the maximal side of things you'll see shoes like Hokas, which tend to have huge amounts of pillowy padding. In between you'll find speciality trail shoes from a large number of vendors, such as Salomon, The North Face, La Sportiva, and Altra (to name just a few).

Socks​

Good socks, as with good shoes, can be the difference between a good race and a shitty race. If the race has any water crossings you'll want socks that dry quickly so your feet don't get waterlogged. If the race is hot you'll want socks that pull sweat away from your feet quickly and easily.

My socks of choice are Drymax socks. Usually when I prepare my feet prior to a race I will liberally code my feet with vaseline prior to pulling my socks on. I know the Drymax literature suggests I should not use lubricants with them, but I am telling you right now I do and it works. So much of what you need to do in ultrarunning is trial and error, and every piece of advice comes with a your-mileage-may-vary caveat. I learned long ago that the best way for me to avoid blisters is to use vaseline with Drymax socks.
