Pace​

How fast you run. Usually we measure this in minutes per mile. In general during a 100 mile race your page will be much slower than what you may be used to during a road race.

F » First Date

Pacer​

A runner who paces another runner. Usually a pacer can start after 50 miles. A runner can generally have any number of pacers, but only one at a time. A pacer will need to start pacing at an aid station, and not anywhere else. Generally a pacer is allowed to partake of anything they want at an aid station, just like their runner.

A pacer's job is generally doing anything necessary to get their runner to the finish line. In addition to running with a runner, a pacer often takes on a number of other duties, such as getting food or drink for their runner at an aid station, helping their runner change shoes, and anything else required to keep the runner moving. It's often a completely thankless job. Your runner will want to sit down and not get up. They will want to drop rather than go one more step. They will throw fits. They may call you names. Keep them moving, no matter what.

Unless they are injured. Let injured runners drop.

One thing a pacer cannot do is mule for their runner. That's off limits.

Pacing​

Running with someone to help them finish a race. See pacer.

P » Pacer

Pack​

A running backpack that lets you carry gear easily on your back. These come in all different shapes and sizes depending on your preference and distance you're traveling. Packs commonly have the ability to store a hydration bladder (I usually called these hydration packs) and usually have a variety of pockets for those odds and ends you might want on a long race, such as chapstick, gloves, a flashlight or headlamp, spare batteries, fuel, poles, sunglasses, etc.

Your choice of pack can have a huge impact on your enjoyment of the race. A poorly fitting pack can bounce, chafe, or otherwise be unpleasant in a large number of ways, while you might forget you're even wearing a well-fitting pack.

Poles​

Long poles you can use when mountain running or hiking to help make climbs easier by taking some of your weight on your arms and shoulders. They can also provide balance in a variety of situations, such as when crossing streams.

They come in a variety of sizes, with the object being you configure the pole length so your elbows are bent at 90 degrees when holding the poles in front of you. They come in different configurations, as well. Some will be a single shaft, while others can collapse for easier stowing in or on a pack, while still others telescope.

Black Diamond Distance Z Trekking Poles are a popular variety of trekking poles. These are an example of poles that collapse.
