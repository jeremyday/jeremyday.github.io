Bib​

A small piece of paper with your race number on it. You'll have to wear this somewhere where aid station volunteers can see it. At most aid stations volunteers will log runners in and out. This is so the race director knows where all the runners are on the course.

Ultra Tip

If you need to drop out of a race you need to notify the aid station captain and give them your bib number.

Blisters​

These will make your life suck. Keep your feet dry, change your socks if necessary, use vaseline or some other lubricant, and you'll go a long way towards preventing blisters.

There are a lot of differing opinions on how to treat blisters mid-race. I've found duct tape over the top of an unpopped blister is usually a pretty good bet. You can find duct tape just about anywhere, making it ideal for races in remote places.

Ultra Tip

Blisters might slow you down but they don't need to end your race.

Bodyglide​

A common brand of...personal lubrication. It comes in a bar that is easy to apply. Some folks use it on their feet, but that doesn't do it for me, as I prefer Vaseline. I have used Bodyglide on my back to prevent chafing from my pack. On the whole it is clean and not greasy, but you need to plan to have some in advance, because it is not the kind of gear you can find just anywhere on short notice.

G » Gear

Buff​

Buff, like Kleenex, is a brand that has become its own product category. A buff is multi-purpose tube of fabric. You can wear it around your neck, on your head like a hat, use it to tie back your hair, or keep it over your mouth if there's a lot of dust. When I race I usually put one around my neck. It keeps the sun off, the sweat (and therefor cool) close, and you can put ice in it to keep you cool.
